# Posting Frequency Strategy — LipeMoves
### Current Plan vs Daily Posting vs What Actually Grows Fastest

---

## The Short Answer

**Don't post every day on feed. Do show up every day on stories.**

Daily feed posting sounds like the fastest path to growth — it's not. It burns you out in week 2, tanks your quality, and the algorithm doesn't reward volume, it rewards *engagement rate*. One reel with 200 comments beats five reels with 10 comments each.

The accounts that grow fastest post **4–5 times per week on feed** with high-quality content and **show up daily on stories** to stay top of mind.

## What the Algorithm Actually Rewards

Instagram's Reels algorithm ranks content by:

1. **Watch rate** — did people watch past 3 seconds?
2. **Completion rate** — did they watch the whole thing?
3. **Saves** — the strongest signal of value
4. **Shares** — sends it to new accounts (reach multiplier)
5. **Comments** — engagement depth

None of these improve by posting more. They improve by making **better content**.

A reel with 80% completion rate posted twice a week will outgrow an account posting mediocre content daily. Every time.

---

## The Daily Posting Trap

Here's what actually happens when creators go daily:

- **Week 1:** High energy, decent quality, good numbers
- **Week 2:** Running out of ideas, filming gets rushed
- **Week 3:** Posting for the sake of posting, engagement drops
- **Week 4:** Burnout. Miss a day. Feel guilty. Quit.

This is the #1 reason fitness creators stop at 1,000 followers.

---

## What Fast-Growing Accounts Actually Do

Accounts that went from 0 to 10k in 3–4 months typically:

- Posted **4–5 reels per week** (not 7)
- Had a **consistent visual style** from day 1
- **Replied to every comment** in the first hour
- Used **carousels as their save magnet** (saves = algorithmic rocket fuel)
- Showed up **daily on stories** — polls, questions, raw clips
- Had a **clear niche hook** that made people hit follow immediately

## Recommended Schedule for Maximum Growth

| Day | Format | Pillar |

| Monday | Reel (30–50s) | Move — drill or flow |
| Tuesday | Carousel (5–7 slides) | Education — save-worthy tips |

| Thursday | — | Rest from feed. Engage only. |

| Sunday | — | Rest. Plan next week. |

### Stories (every single day, 3–5 slides)

| Time | What to post |
|---|---|
| Morning | A thought, question, or poll |
| During/after training | Raw clip — unedited, real |
| Evening | Tip, saved post, or a question for followers |

Stories don't need to be produced. Phone up, train, post. That's it.

## The TikTok Exception

TikTok is the one platform where **daily posting does help** in the beginning, because:
- TikTok's algorithm tests every video with a cold audience
- More at-bats = faster learning what resonates
- The bar for production quality is lower

**Strategy:** batch record your Instagram reels, then on the leftover days post **raw, unedited TikTok-native content** — talking to camera, in-the-moment clips, real training sessions.

So your actual output looks like:
- **Instagram:** 4–5 polished posts/week
- **TikTok:** 5–7 posts/week (includes raw content)
- **YouTube Shorts:** 3–4/week (repurpose best IG reels)

## Content Batching System (How to Make This Sustainable)

### One filming session per week = all your content

**Sunday or Monday — 2 to 3 hours:**
- Film 3 reels back to back (change outfit between if needed)
- Film 1 raw talking-head for TikTok
- Film B-roll (hands, feet, equipment close-ups)

**Tuesday — 1 hour:**
- Edit all reels in CapCut
- Build 1–2 carousels in Canva
- Write all captions

**Wednesday — 30 min:**
- Schedule everything in Later or Buffer
- Done for the week

Then all that's left is **daily stories** (5 minutes) and **daily engagement** (20–30 minutes of commenting).

## The One Thing That Changes Everything

More important than posting frequency is **the hook**.

The first 2 seconds of your reel determine 80% of your reach. A perfect reel posted 3x/week with strong hooks will always outperform 7 mediocre reels per week.

So before you ask "should I post more?" — ask "is my hook stopping the scroll?"
